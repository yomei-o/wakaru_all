01Place a force as a "rotation on the lattice"

First, discretize spacetime into a lattice (sites, links, faces). Then place the gauge field, not on the sites, but as a group element \(U_\mu(x)\in G\) living on each link (the edge crossing from one site to the neighboring one). This is a rotation representing "how much the internal orientation (color / phase) turns when you cross one step of the lattice."

Gauge field = a rotation on a link
$$U_\mu(x)=\exp\!\big(i\,a\,g\,A_\mu(x)\big)\in G$$

\(a\) = lattice spacing, \(g\) = coupling, \(A_\mu\) = gauge field, \(G\) = group. One rotation \(U\) per link.

02One formula ── Wilson's plaquette action

The rule of the force (the action) can be written astonishingly simply. It's determined by nothing but the product of rotations around the smallest square loop (the plaquette \(\square\)). How much the loop "fails to close" is the field's curvature = the strength of the force.

The Wilson action (1974) ── this is the "one formula"
$$S=\beta\sum_{\square}\Big[\,1-\tfrac{1}{N}\,\mathrm{Re}\,\mathrm{Tr}\,U_\square\,\Big],\qquad U_\square=U_\mu(x)\,U_\nu(x{+}\mu)\,U_\mu^{\dagger}(x{+}\nu)\,U_\nu^{\dagger}(x)$$

\(U_\square\) = the product going once around the plaquette's four links. \(\beta=2N/g^2\). That's all.

03Changing the force = "just change the group"

Here's the crux. Keep the formula \(S=\beta\sum_\square[\cdots]\) completely unchanged in form and just swap the group \(G\), and the three forces fit into a single line.

The three forces are not separate "mystery constants" but the same Yang–Mills structure with different groups ── this is what genuine unification looks like (the Standard Model's gauge group \(SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1)\)). Whereas the numerology of \(1/(Cn)^D\) tuned the exponents by hand, here they are fixed uniquely by the symmetry that is the group.

04In the continuum limit, back to familiar Yang–Mills

The lattice is a "scaffold." As you shrink the lattice spacing (\(a\to0\)), the plaquette action goes back to the familiar continuum Yang–Mills.

The continuum limit ── remove the scaffold $$S=\beta\sum_\square\big[1-\tfrac1N\mathrm{Re\,Tr}\,U_\square\big]\ \xrightarrow{\ a\to0\ }\ \frac{1}{4}\int d^4x\,F_{\mu\nu}^a F^{a\,\mu\nu}$$

The plaquette's "failure to close" becomes exactly the field strength \(F_{\mu\nu}\) (curvature). The discrete correctly contains the continuum.

And ── this discrete formula is the very tool that actually computes the mass gap of Episode 4. Strongly-coupled QCD, which diverges and is intractable in the continuum, could be solved on a supercomputer only once put on a lattice, yielding the proton mass, confinement, and the glueball mass gap. Your aim, "the four forces in one discrete formula," met the story of the mass gap in the very same single formula.

An honest line (recovering Bonus ①) Lattice gauge theory does not claim "the universe is discrete." The lattice is a regularization (scaffold) that preserves gauge invariance, and the physics is the continuum limit \(a\to0\). Mistaking the scaffold for reality is the trap of Bonus ①. If you want to make discreteness fundamental, the Lorentz-invariant-preserving causal set is a candidate.

An honest line

This installment is pure quantum field theory / quantum gravity, unrelated to the watchword \(c\cdot t=\text{constant}\) (the lattice is a regularization, not coordinates). Lattice gauge theory is rock-solid as the unification of the three gauge forces, but it is not by itself a "theory of everything" ── the integration of gravity (spin foams, etc.) is unfinished, and why the group is \(SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1)\) and why the couplings have their values are also unsolved (grand unification, the homework of Episode 6, part one).

"Is discreteness fundamental?" is also unsettled. The lattice is a scaffold (the physics is the continuum limit); causal sets are a candidate for fundamental discreteness but a frontier. Not asserting is the honest line.

    See the answer
    All three have the same Yang–Mills structure (group element \(U\) on links, plaquette action \(S=\beta\sum_\square[1-\frac1N\mathrm{Re\,Tr}\,U_\square]\)), and the only difference is the gauge group \(G\) (SU(3)/SU(2)/U(1)). The action's "form" is identical, and the group determines each force's individuality.
  2. Does lattice gauge theory claim that "the universe is discrete"?
    See the answer
    No. The lattice is a regularization (scaffold) to preserve gauge invariance, and the physics is the continuum limit \(a\to0\) (Episode 4, Bonus ①). Treating discreteness as fundamental is a separate claim, in which case Lorentz-preserving causal sets and the like become candidates.
  3. Why doesn't gravity fit smoothly into the same formula?
    See the answer
    Because gravity's coupling \(G\) is dimensionful and non-renormalizable (the Finale). There are attempts to write it in "the same discrete language" via lattice gauge theory of the Lorentz group (spin foams) or Regge calculus, but the continuum limit and the recovery of general relativity are an unsolved frontier.
